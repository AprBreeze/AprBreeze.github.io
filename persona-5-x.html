Design Intent

Three goals drove every layout and beat decision:

  • Scale & tension — Make the Boss feel massive and the shrunken party genuinely vulnerable
  • Thematic clarity — Use the gift pile to express vanity and overindulgence (the enemy spoiled by her parents)
  • Experience vs. cost — Balance visual impact, clean camera coverage, narrative service, and interactive traversal without scope blowout

Greybox Iteration

The first greybox already tried to control cost, but the flow stayed structurally complex. Art polish and camera validation would have consumed disproportionate time.

After syncing with art on tight resourcing, I led a second pass: simplified layout complexity, reused existing assets wherever possible, and kept the core beats: boss proximity, stealth tension, thematic read, and traversal interaction.

  • V1 — Full-flow proof of concept; validated pacing but flagged high art cost
  • V2 — Production-ready simplification; same player experience, lower build risk

Narrative, Dialogue & Cinematics

Beyond layout, I wrote character dialogue for the sequence and worked with narrative to polish lines and pacing. I also choreographed cinematic beats: timing, blocking, and camera intent alongside our internal cinematics team.

I did the final validation with my manager before handing off to Atlas Japan for final review before shipping.

This concludes the complete production loop:
Ideation → Blockout → Art → Narrative/Cinematic → Approval → Live (Reach the players!!)

End-to-End Pipeline

  • Greybox v2 → simplify structure, maximize asset reuse

Persona 5: The Phantom X — Side Quest Design

Part 1 — Overview

On Persona 5: The Phantom X, I worked as a level designer on three side quests inside the Chapter 4 palace: a narrative puzzle in the Girl's Room, a mechanism loop puzzle, and a long exploration quest in the Private Domain area.

I owned each one from puzzle logic through dialogue, camera, and final configuration.

Quest scope — Chapter 4 Palace

Part 2 — Design Thinking

2.1 Starting From What the Player Needs

Before I touch a single mechanic, I try to name what the player actually needs in that moment. In P5X that meant light cognitive load paired with narrative texture, since side quests exist to give players a breather between main story beats, not to test their patience.

I research comparable puzzle references, then translate that target into a concrete design goal before sketching anything. That target became my filter for later decisions, including which mechanics to cut when they got too expensive to feel worth it.

2.2 Building Puzzles Out of Modules

I build small, self-contained puzzle modules first, then assemble them into the full quest. Each module solves one idea on its own, which makes it easy to test in isolation, swap out if it doesn't land, or reuse elsewhere.

This habit paid off most on the longer quests, where a single room needing rework didn't mean reworking the whole sequence.

Modular assembly

2.3 Script Before Space

I write dialogue and narrative beats before laying out the physical space. Text is the cheapest thing to revise, and writing it first forces me to know what feeling a room is supporting before I commit to its geometry.

Every quest followed the same order: script, then space, then mechanics, then iteration, each stage getting more expensive to change than the last.

Production order & rising cost of change

2.4 Designing Behavior Tree Logic

Mechanisms in P5X talk to each other through behavior trees, and a chunk of my time went into untangling that logic: selectors, sequences, parallel branches, and how a change in one node ripples into the next.

A representative example: a puzzle where lighting the correct lantern color sends a guard away, which only then increments a chest's interaction count from zero to one, unlocking it. Cases like that taught me to think in states and transitions, not just trigger and effect.

Behavior tree logic (square = condition, diamond = selector)

2.5 Letting Testing Find What the Document Couldn't

Every quest taught me something during playtesting that the design document hadn't surfaced. A floating object needed a physical platform once art got involved.

A puzzle state read as a bug instead of a clue until I rewrote the failure feedback to be specific instead of vague. I treat early playtests as the real first draft of a puzzle, not a final check.

Playtesting feedback loop

Part 3 — What Stuck With Me

  • Remind, not recall. Tell the player what a tool does the moment they need it, instead of expecting them to remember a rule from ten minutes earlier.
  • Separate what can't change from what can. Cross-department work goes smoothly when I'm clear about my non-negotiables and leave everything else open to other teams' expertise.
  • Design isn't validated until someone else plays it. The clearest problems and the best ideas both showed up during testing, not at my desk.
  • One task at a time. A sticky note system that lives outside my screen does more for my focus than trying to multitask inside it.
