Quick Summary

NuToast is a global B2B nut ingredient supplier. Despite steady website traffic, very few visitors converted into sales inquiries. We ran a full-cycle UX redesign: research, information architecture, visual design, WordPress implementation, and usability testing — all within 12 weeks.

Phase 1: Empathize

Business Problem

The site had traffic. It didn't have conversions.

2,413 avg. monthly visitors
40% left without passing the homepage
1 in 5 reached the contact page
Despite steady traffic, NuToast's website wasn't converting visitors into leads. Procurement buyers — the primary audience — were dropping off before they could evaluate a single product or reach the contact page.

Current Issues

IA AUDIT

The site buries what buyers need most

01

Product information was buried

Product categories sit below events, best sellers, about us, and a promo video on the homepage. A procurement manager scrolling to evaluate product scope has to get past 5 unrelated sections first.

02

Trust signals were hidden

Certifications and production & quality control info are limited to the About page, with certificates placed too low to be noticed. They are missing from the homepage and product pages, the two places procurements actually go to evaluate a supplier.

03

Content was misplaced

Product pages include unrelated content like company FAQs, while About Us appears on the homepage. Information is not organized around what buyers need at each stage, making it harder to find what matters.

Users' Voices

USER INTERVIEWS

Do these problems actually matter?

We interviewed procurement and sales professionals to validate whether the IA issues we found affect real buying decisions.

PARTICIPANTS

  • 3 procurements
  • 5 NuToast sales

SCREENER

  • 3–5 year experience in B2B sourcing / sales

FORMAT

  • 45 minutes
  • Remote on Zoom

This is what we found:

1

Product fit is the first thing buyers check

8/8 participants

Buyers ask "do they make what I need?" before anything else. Then they filter by specs, MOQ, and lead time. If any of this is not immediately clear, they leave.

"I started by checking whether the product meets our needs. review the product catalog first, use filters to narrow down, then check specs, lead time, and MOQ."—P4
2

Credibility and supply stability make or break the deal

8/8 participants

Buyers need proof a supplier is legitimate and reliable before they'll initiate contact. Such proof includes years in operation, scale, certifications, production capacity, QA/QC processes, and brand collaborations.

"Customers often need long-term, stable supply… If they can clearly see our factory capabilities…and certifications, they are more willing to communicate further with us."—P1
2

Sales needs structured context to respond fast

8/8 participants

Sales staff said most inquiries arrive with too little context: no product interest, no estimated order volume. They spend time chasing basics before they can qualify the lead.

"First, we need to understand the customer…and what direction they're working in, and then we prepare matching samples." "We really need their purchasing volume… It's used to assess their scale."—P1
✓

Conclusion

Yes, every problem matters. All 3 IA issues map directly to real buyer behaviors.

How Others Do It

COMPETITOR Analysis

How do others solve this?

We audited three competitors to identify patterns that work in B2B ingredient sourcing: what to adopt and what to adapt for NuToast.

Callebaut

Best-in-class confectionery ingredient leader

MEC3

Adjacent competitor, overlapping buyer base

Master Martini

Adjacent competitor, broader ingredient portfolio

Here are the key patterns across all three competitors:

You can see what they sell right away

All three show their product categories early on the homepage using large, visual cards. A buyer knows within seconds if the supplier carries what they need.

Credibility shows up everywhere, not just one page

Heritage & expertise, production & QA appear across the homepage, product pages, and about page, wherever a buyer might need reassurance.

Phase 3: Design

Design Interventions

Each decision maps directly to a validated research finding.

Objective 01

Make it obvious what NuToast sells

Homepage:product categories
BEFORE AFTER
  • Product categories placed too low on the page
  • Product cards are static with no links to product pages
  • Product categories shown right below the hero
  • Each card links directly to a product page
Homepage:best seller
BEFORE AFTER
  • Users don't know this is a best-selling product
  • Product information is scattered, hard to scan
  • Doesn't guide users on what to do next
  • Uses a distinct header and a trophy icon to indicate a best seller
  • Key highlights are grouped for quick scanning
  • CTAs guide users to explore or request a quote
Product:product listing
BEFORE AFTER
  • Filters are small and tightly stacked, making them easy to miss
  • Only one filter can be selected at a time
  • Uses larger visual category tabs as the main navigation
  • Multi-select filters to support flexible narrowing
Product:product cards
BEFORE AFTER
  • Product names are long and hard to scan
  • Key attributes are buried in the name
  • Every card uses a "Read more" button, adding visual clutter
  • Uses short product names with key attributes shown as tags
  • Cards are fully clickable, removing the need for a separate CTA
Product:product details
BEFORE AFTER
  • Key specs are buried in dense paragraphs
  • No clear application context to show how the product is used
  • Surfaces key specs in the hero using scannable bullet cards
  • Adds application photos to show real use cases
Product:product specs
BEFORE AFTER
  • Technical specs buried in long paragraphs
  • Specs structured into a clear parameter table
Objective 02

Build trust where buyers actually look

Homepage:Hero
BEFORE AFTER
  • Logo and tagline only: no indication of scale, experience, or why a buyer should keep scrolling
  • Headline states what NuToast does and who it's for, backed by key numbers
Homepage:First-Scroll
BEFORE AFTER
  • Buyers have no clear sense of who NuToast serves or how its products are used.
  • Customer segments shown as labeled chips paired with a scrolling gallery of real client product applications.

Section not present in original design

Homepage:Industry Presence
BEFORE AFTER
  • Buyers cannot quickly tell if NuToast operates at their scale or serves their region.
  • Map showing global export footprint
  • Company figures displayed as large, scannable figures alongside the map

Section not present in original design

Homepage:How We Work
BEFORE AFTER
  • No clear explanation of how NuToast sources, processes, and delivers products
  • Buyers can't see how quality and supply are actually ensured
  • Gives a quick overview of NuToast's process from sourcing to delivery
  • A more detailed version is placed on the About page for deeper review

Section not present in original design

About:Company Overview
BEFORE AFTER
  • Company credibility and positioning content are misplaced within product pages, making them easy to miss or misinterpret.
  • Consolidated company introduction into a dedicated "Why NuToast" section at the top of the About page, combining value proposition, capabilities, and key proof points.
  • Paired with a visual carousel of products and factory imagery.
About:Certifications
BEFORE AFTER
  • Compliance section buried low on the page.
  • Certs displayed as a cluster of overlapping images, hard to read or verify.
  • Certification logos listed but don't link to actual documents.
  • Compliance section moved higher on the page
  • Each cert shown in its own large, full-size card, not thumbnails
  • Clicking any tab on the right switches the left panel to show that cert in full detail
About:Production line images
BEFORE AFTER
  • Production images lack visual prominence
  • Presented as random, disconnected visuals without clear context, factory images feel like decoration, not evidence
  • Structured production & traceability narrative
  • Factory images serve as proof, showing the actual facility, production lines, and on-site QA with a description explaining the process end to end
About:Operational Support
BEFORE AFTER
  • Buyers don't know how NuToast handles fulfillment, turnaround time, or international requirements in practice.
  • Outlines how NuToast handles fulfillment, customization, documentation, and global delivery, supported by concrete metrics such as product availability and sample turnaround.
  • Placed at the end of the About page to complete the company capability narrative.

Section not present in original design

Homepage:Application Support
BEFORE AFTER
  • Does not provide any additional information beyond the products themselves.
  • Added certification logos directly below the product specs

Objective 03

Collect the right information upfront

Contact:contact form
BEFORE AFTER
  • Only collects basic contact info plus a free-text message box with no guidance on what to write
  • Map and factory tour info compete for attention
  • Form adds structured fields: role, region, product interest, estimated order volume
  • Other services clearly separated below the form

WordPress Implementation

We didn't stop at a prototype. The full redesign was built as a live WordPress site on a staging environment, with the complete design system implemented across all pages.

Impact

Product visibility

Procurement buyers can now assess product fit within seconds of landing on the homepage, the single biggest factor in whether they stay or leave.

Credibility where it matters

Trust signals are now distributed across the homepage, product listing, and product detail pages instead of confined to one About page.

Sales-ready inquiries

Every contact form submission arrives with structured context, role, product interest, order volume, so sales can qualify and respond without chasing basics.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

Recruit external procurement participants for usability testing rather than relying on sales staff as a proxy. Sales understand buyer behavior well, but direct validation with procurement would strengthen the findings.

What this project confirmed

Research-driven design decisions are easier to defend and easier to implement. When every change traces back to a validated finding, the rationale is already built in.
